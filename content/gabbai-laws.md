# Laws and Requirements for the Gabbai / הֲלָכוֹת הַגַּבַּאי

## Who May Serve as Gabbai

The gabbai (*gabbai tzedakah* or *gabbai ha-kehillah*) is a communal officer responsible for maintaining order and dignity during synagogue services. While the role is not strictly a halachic office in the same sense as a *chazan* or *posek*, those who assume it take on real obligations.

### Minimum Qualifications

A gabbai must be:

- A Jewish adult male (*bar mitzvah* and above)[^gb1]
- Of sound mind and able to conduct his duties reliably[^gb2]
- Familiar with the order of the service and the laws of Torah reading[^gb3]

Some authorities require that a gabbai be personally Shabbat-observant, since he exercises authority over the sacred service and publicly represents the congregation.[^gb4]

### Character and Reputation

The Talmud (*Bava Batra* 8b) establishes that communal officials, including those who oversee charity funds (*gabbaei tzedakah*), must be of known good character and trusted by the community.[^gb5] This principle has been extended to the synagogue gabbai: he should be respected, honest, and tactful — capable of distributing honours without causing offence.[^gb6]

One who is suspected of dishonesty should not be appointed as gabbai of funds, and by extension, one who is known to show systematic favouritism should not serve as gabbai of aliyot.[^gb7]

## Appointment and Term of Office

A gabbai is appointed by the community (*kahal*) or its leadership. He should not appoint himself.[^gb8] Two gabbaim acting together — so that one checks the other — is the preferred arrangement for any communal role involving honours or funds.[^gb9]

Once appointed, a gabbai may not be removed from office without cause, as removal from a position of honour brings shame (*bizayon*).[^gb10] If the gabbai neglects his duties or acts improperly, the community may reassign him.

## Duties During the Torah Reading

### Knowing the Names

The gabbai is responsible for knowing the Hebrew names (including father's name) of those receiving aliyot before the service begins.[^gb11] He should confirm names beforehand and not guess or improvise during the reading, as announcing an incorrect name is embarrassing for the oleh.[^gb12]

### Maintaining Order

The gabbai must ensure that aliyot are distributed with proper dignity. He must be aware of:

- Who is a Kohen, Levi, or Yisrael
- Who has precedence (mourners, bar mitzvah, chatan, yahrzeit, etc.)
- The correct number of aliyot for that day[^gb13]

### Correcting the Ba'al Korei

The gabbai follows the Torah reading in a *tikkun* and is responsible for correcting the reader (*ba'al korei*) when a significant error (*taut me'akev*) occurs — one that changes the meaning of the text.[^gb14] He must be familiar enough with the text to distinguish significant errors from minor mispronunciations.

If the gabbai himself makes an error — for instance, failing to correct a mistake that should have been corrected — the congregation should inform him.[^gb15]

### Responsibility for the Torah Scroll

The gabbai is responsible for the honour and safety of the Torah scroll during the service. He should ensure:

- The scroll is dressed (*gartel*, mantle, breastplate, crown) properly before and after reading[^gb16]
- The scroll is not left unattended while open[^gb17]
- The scroll is lifted (*hagbah*) and wrapped (*gelilah*) by individuals who can perform these honours respectfully[^gb18]

## Distributing Aliyot and Honours

### The Obligation to Distribute Fairly

The gabbai has an obligation to distribute synagogue honours fairly and without playing personal favourites beyond established halachic priorities.[^gb19] Withholding an aliyah from someone who has a legitimate claim — such as a mourner or a *bar mitzvah* — without good reason is considered a slight both to the individual and to the dignity of the Torah.[^gb20]

At the same time, the gabbai has discretion to maintain communal peace (*shalom ha-tzibbur*). If calling a particular person would cause a dispute or embarrassment, he may redirect the honour to another.[^gb21]

### Selling Aliyot

In many communities aliyot are sold or auctioned, particularly on Yom Tov. The gabbai oversees this process. Funds raised typically go to the synagogue or to charity. The gabbai must ensure that the sale does not disturb the dignity of the service.[^gb22]

::: note-chabad
**Chabad custom:** The practice of selling aliyot through public auction on Yom Tov is not the Chabad custom. Aliyot are distributed by the gabbai according to communal need and priority, without a public sale. Pledges (*neder*) made at the Torah are still recorded and donated.[^gb23]
:::

::: note-sephardic
**Sephardic custom:** Auctioning aliyot on Yom Tov is widely practiced in Sephardic communities. The *Kaf HaChayyim* discusses the correct procedure and notes that the auction itself, when conducted with decorum, adds to the honour of the Torah.[^gb24]
:::

### Cannot Refuse an Oleh

Once an individual has been publicly called to the Torah (*ya'amod*), the gabbai may not rescind the call without strong justification, as this would publicly embarrass the person.[^gb25] If the gabbai called the wrong person by mistake, the announced oleh should still come up if he is halachically eligible.[^gb26]

## The Gabbai's Personal Conduct

### Standing During the Reading

The gabbai should stand throughout the Torah reading, out of respect for the Torah.[^gb27] He should not sit, lean, or engage in conversation while the Torah is being read.

### Attention During the Reading

The gabbai must pay attention to the reading — both to correct errors and to follow the text. Conversing during the Torah reading is prohibited for all congregants, and the gabbai sets an example.[^gb28]

::: note-chabad
**Chabad custom:** It is specifically noted in Chabad practice that the gabbai should not speak at all during the Torah reading, even in whispers. Any communication with the oleh or the ba'al korei should be done by gesture or written note if possible.[^gb29]
:::

### Receiving an Aliyah

A gabbai may receive an aliyah himself, but should be careful not to monopolise the honours he distributes. Some authorities recommend that he call himself modestly and without self-promotion.[^gb30]

### Respect for the Rabbi and Community Leadership

The gabbai must defer to the rabbi on matters of halachic dispute that arise during the service. If the rabbi rules one way on a question of eligibility or procedure, the gabbai must follow that ruling even if he disagrees.[^gb31]

## Communal Funds (*Gabbai Tzedakah*)

Where the same individual serves as both the synagogue gabbai and the overseer of communal charity funds, additional obligations apply. The *gabbai tzedakah* must:

- Collect funds faithfully and disperse them to the eligible poor[^gb32]
- Not make decisions about large disbursements alone — a second gabbai or community representative should participate[^gb33]
- Keep accurate records[^gb34]
- Not benefit personally from the funds[^gb35]

The laws of *gabbai tzedakah* are detailed in *Yoreh Deah* 256–259 and represent one of the most trusted roles in halachic communal governance.
