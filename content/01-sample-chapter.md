# The Torah Reading / קְרִיאַת הַתּוֹרָה

## Overview

The public reading of the Torah (*keriat ha-Torah*) is performed from a handwritten parchment scroll (*sefer Torah*).  The gabbai is responsible for coordinating every aspect of the reading, from removing the scroll from the ark to its return.

## The Number of Aliyot

The number of Torah portions (*aliyot*) called varies by day. The following table documents the standard number of aliyot for the day/category.  It also notes (with +1) when the extra maftir/מַפְטִיר reading is added (for the person who will read the haftarah/הַפְטָרָה).  A gabbai may also call additional aliyot under certain circumstances ([TBD]).

| Day                                  | Number of *Aliyot* |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------ |
| Shabbat Morning                      | 7 + 1              |
| Yom Kippur Morning                   | 6 + 1              |
| Yom Tov (three Pilgrimage Festivals) | 5                  |
| Rosh Hashanah                        | 5                  |
| Rosh Chodesh / Chol Ha-Moed          | 4                  |
| Monday / Thursday / Shabbat Mincha   | 3                  |
| Yom Kippur Mincha                    | 2 + 1              |

## Calling an *Oleh* / קְרִיאַת הָעוֹלֶה

When calling a person to the Torah, the gabbai announces the *aliyah* in Hebrew.  The traditional formula includes the person's Hebrew name and father's name (*ben* for a man, *bat* for a woman):

יַעֲמֹד [שֵׁם] בֶּן/בַּת [שֵׁם הָאָב] לַעֲלִיָּה [מִסְפַּר]

The gabbai should know in advance the Hebrew names of those receiving honours.  It is good practice to confirm names before the service.

## Correcting Errors

The gabbai must follow the reading carefully in a *tikkun* (a printed book with both the vowelised and unvowelised text side by side) and correct any significant error (*taut*) by the reader (*ba'al korei*).

An error is considered significant (*me'akev*) if it changes the meaning of the text.  Minor mispronunciations that do not affect meaning need not be corrected publicly, though the gabbai may quietly guide the reader.
