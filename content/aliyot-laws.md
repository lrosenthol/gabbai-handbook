# Laws of Calling Up to the Torah / הֲלָכוֹת עֲלִיַּת הַתּוֹרָה

## Who Is Eligible for an Aliyah

Any male Jew who has reached the age of majority (*bar mitzvah*, thirteen years and one day) is eligible to receive an aliyah.[^al1] A minor may be called for *maftir* on Shabbat and Yom Tov in extenuating circumstances, but not for a numbered weekday aliyah.[^al2]

A person who is deaf-mute (*cheresh*) in both hearing and speech, or who lacks mental capacity (*shoteh*), may not receive an aliyah because they cannot recite the blessings meaningfully.[^al3] One who is deaf but can speak, or mute but can hear, follows a lenient ruling in most communities.[^al4]

### Kohen, Levi, and Yisrael

The first aliyah is given to a *Kohen*, the second to a *Levi*, and all remaining aliyot to *Yisraelim*.[^al5] This order maintains the honour of the Torah and reflects the biblical distinctions of the priestly and levitical classes.

If no Kohen is present, a Levi may receive the first aliyah, or alternatively a Yisrael may be called and announced as though no Kohen is present (*ein kan Kohen*). A Levi should not receive the first aliyah in place of an absent Kohen unless it is announced; otherwise it would appear a slight to the Kohanic dignity.[^al6]

If no Levi is present, the Kohen who received the first aliyah repeats for the second, announced as *b'makom Levi*.[^al7]

If a Kohen is present but the gabbai wishes to call a non-Kohen (e.g., for a *bar mitzvah* boy who is a Yisrael), the Kohen must be formally asked to leave the synagogue or declare that he waives his precedence (*mechilah*) before the first aliyah is called.[^al8]

::: note-chabad
**Chabad custom (Lubavitch):** The Rebbe (Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson) ruled that it is preferable that the Kohen himself declare his waiver (*mechilah*) aloud before the first aliyah is called, and that asking him to leave the room is not the preferred method. The Kohen remains present but explicitly yields precedence.[^al9]
:::

### Precedence Among Yisraelim

Among Yisraelim, the following individuals have precedence for receiving an aliyah, listed from highest to lowest:

1. A *bar mitzvah* boy on the Shabbat of his bar mitzvah[^al10]
2. A bridegroom (*chatan*) on the Shabbat before or after his wedding[^al11]
3. A mourner (*avel*) within the first year of bereavement — custom varies[^al12]
4. A father whose son is to be circumcised (*brit milah*) that week[^al13]
5. A person observing a *yahrzeit*[^al14]
6. A person who has just returned from a journey at sea or across the desert, recovered from a serious illness, or been released from captivity (*bentching gomel*)[^al15]

These precedences are social conventions (*minhag*) and may be adjusted by the gabbai to maintain communal peace (*shalom ha-tzibbur*).

## Disqualified Individuals

### Public Violations (*Mechalel Shabbat Befarhesya*)

One who publicly desecrates the Shabbat — meaning regularly and openly, in a manner known to the community — should not be called to the Torah according to many *poskim*.[^al16] This is a sensitive matter and the gabbai should consult the rabbi of the community for guidance rather than act unilaterally.

### Apostates and Non-Jews

An apostate (*meshumad*) who has converted to another religion is not called to the Torah.[^al17] A non-Jew may not receive an aliyah under any circumstances.[^al18]

### A Person Under *Cherem*

One placed under communal ban (*cherem*) should not receive an aliyah until the ban is lifted.[^al19]

## Calling the Oleh — the Announcement Formula

The gabbai calls each person to the Torah with a standard Hebrew announcement. The traditional Ashkenazic formula is:

יַעֲמֹד [שֵׁם] בֶּן [שֵׁם הָאָב] לַעֲלִיַּת [שְׁנִי / שְׁלִישִׁי / …]

For the first aliyah (*Kohen*):

יַעֲמֹד [שֵׁם] בֶּן [שֵׁם הָאָב] הַכֹּהֵן לַעֲלִיַּת רִאשׁוֹן

For the second (*Levi*):

יַעֲמֹד [שֵׁם] בֶּן [שֵׁם הָאָב] הַלֵּוִי לַעֲלִיַּת שֵׁנִי

The name of the oleh is announced using his Hebrew name and his father's Hebrew name.[^al20] If his father's name is unknown, the name of the mother is used.[^al21]

::: note-sephardic
**Sephardic custom:** Many Sephardic communities use the formula *ya'amod* followed only by the person's Hebrew name, without announcing the ordinal number of the aliyah. The aliyah number is considered already known to the congregation. Some communities also add the phrase *v'yevarech* ("and may he bless").[^al22]
:::

::: note-chabad
**Chabad custom:** The Chabad announcement follows the Ashkenazic formula but typically adds *Yaamod* at the start and concludes *rishon / sheni / …* before *la'aliyah*. The precise wording was standardised in Chabad siddurim.[^al23]
:::

### Using the Mother's Name in the Announcement

Under normal circumstances the father's name is used. In cases where the father's name is not known — such as a convert (*ger*), a foundling (*asuf*), or where the father is not Jewish — the mother's name is used.[^al24]

Some communities use the mother's name for all calls when a person is ill or when reciting a *mi sheberach* for healing; this is a later custom (*minhag acharonim*) without strict halachic requirement.[^al25]

## The Aliyah Itself

### Approaching the Torah

The oleh should approach the *bimah* promptly.[^al26] He should take the shorter path up and the longer path down, to show eagerness to perform the mitzvah.[^al27] Some authorities require him to use a gartel (sash) if he is not already wearing one.[^al28]

### Looking at the Place in the Scroll

Before reciting the blessing, the oleh opens the scroll and looks at the place where reading will begin. He should not begin the blessing until he has located the text.[^al29]

### Reciting the Blessings

The oleh recites the blessing before the reading (*brachah rishonah*) and the blessing after the reading (*brachah achronah*). He must recite them aloud, and the congregation responds *Amen*.[^al30]

If the oleh began reciting the blessing before the scroll was pointed to the correct place, he does not repeat the blessing.[^al31]

### Remaining at the Bimah

After his portion is read, the oleh must remain standing at the bimah until the next oleh has finished his opening blessing.[^al32] He should not return to his seat until the next oleh's blessing is complete, as it would appear as though he is walking away from the Torah disrespectfully.

::: note-sephardic
**Sephardic custom:** Many Sephardic communities permit the oleh to return to his seat immediately after completing his closing blessing, before the next person comes up. This is the ruling of the *Ben Ish Chai*.[^al33]
:::

### Holding the Torah

The oleh holds the two handles (*atzei chayyim*) of the scroll during the blessings. He should not hold the parchment itself with bare hands.[^al34]

## Special Situations

### A Person Who Cannot Read Hebrew

An oleh who cannot follow the reading is still permitted to receive an aliyah, provided he recites the blessings correctly. The ba'al korei (Torah reader) reads on his behalf.[^al35] However, *lechatchilah* (ideally) one should not call someone who cannot follow along at all, if it can be avoided.[^al36]

### Two Brothers or a Father and Son in Succession

Two brothers, or a father and son, should not receive consecutive aliyot, to avoid the *ayin hara* (evil eye). Some authorities extend this to any two close relatives.[^al37] In small communities where this is unavoidable, the custom is more lenient.[^al38]

### Skipping an Aliyah

The gabbai should not skip aliyot — for example, calling a third and fifth aliyah while omitting the fourth. If an aliyah was accidentally skipped and not noticed until later, the readings already performed are not invalidated, but the skipped aliyah must be made up if possible.[^al39]

### Maftir

Maftir is treated as a separate aliyah. The maftir section slightly overlaps with the final aliyah (*shevi'i* on Shabbat), repeating the last several verses.[^al40] This was instituted so that those called for maftir would not appear to be receiving a lesser honour.[^al41]

A Kohen or Levi may receive maftir without displacing the regular sequence, since maftir is an addition rather than one of the seven required aliyot.[^al42]
